# Reflection on Music Recommender Evaluation

## Profile Comparisons

### High-Energy Pop vs Chill Lofi
The High-Energy Pop profile (favoring pop genre, happy mood, and 0.9 energy level) produces recommendations dominated by upbeat pop tracks like "Sunrise City" (score 4.84 with perfect genre and mood matches plus strong energy alignment) and "Dance Fever" (score 3.96 with genre match and near-perfect energy). In contrast, the Chill Lofi profile (lofi genre, chill mood, 0.4 energy, and acoustic preference) shifts entirely to low-energy, acoustic lofi songs such as "Midnight Coding" (score 5.46 with genre, mood, energy, and acoustic bonuses) and "Library Rain" (score 5.40 with similar matches). What changed dramatically is the energy level—high-energy pop favors tracks around 0.8-0.9 energy with danceable vibes, while chill lofi prioritizes 0.3-0.4 energy tracks that are mellow and acoustic. This makes sense because energy is the most weighted factor (doubled in scoring), so even with genre mismatches, low-energy songs score highly for the chill profile. The acoustic bonus further boosts lofi tracks, creating a clear separation that validates the system's sensitivity to energy preferences and demonstrates how a single preference can override genre matching.

### High-Energy Pop vs Deep Intense Rock
Both the High-Energy Pop (pop, happy, 0.9 energy) and Deep Intense Rock (rock, intense, 0.95 energy) profiles have very high energy preferences, yet their recommendations diverge significantly due to genre and mood priorities. High-energy pop recommends pop-centric tracks like "Sunrise City" (pop happy) and "Dance Fever" (pop party), emphasizing happy, danceable vibes. Deep intense rock, however, favors rock tracks like "Storm Runner" (rock intense, score 4.92 with genre and mood matches) and "Thunder Clap" (rock aggressive), focusing on intense, aggressive energy. What changed is the genre weighting—pop profile gets +2 points for pop matches, rock profile for rock matches, leading to different top songs despite similar energy levels (0.9 vs 0.95). This makes sense because genre carries the highest weight, creating "filter bubbles" where users are locked into their preferred genre even with matching energy. The mood difference (happy vs intense) also plays a role, as "Gym Hero" (pop intense) appears in rock recommendations due to mood match, showing the system correctly balances multiple factors but prioritizes genre.

### Chill Lofi vs Deep Intense Rock
The Chill Lofi profile (lofi, chill, 0.4 energy, acoustic) and Deep Intense Rock profile (rock, intense, 0.95 energy) represent opposite ends of the energy spectrum, resulting in completely different recommendation sets. Chill lofi recommends calm, acoustic tracks like "Midnight Coding" (lofi chill, score 5.46) and "Library Rain" (lofi chill, score 5.40), while deep intense rock suggests high-energy rock like "Storm Runner" (rock intense, score 4.92) and "Thunder Clap" (rock aggressive). What changed fundamentally is the energy preference—low (0.4) vs high (0.95)—causing the system to rank songs based on energy proximity rather than genre. For chill lofi, even non-lofi songs like "Coffee Shop Stories" (jazz) score decently (2.44) due to energy match and acoustic bonus, while for rock, pop songs like "Gym Hero" appear due to intense mood. This makes sense because energy similarity is doubled in weight, making it the dominant factor when preferences are extreme. The acoustic preference in chill lofi adds another layer, rewarding mellow tracks, while rock's lack of acoustic bonus keeps it focused on raw energy. These differences validate that the recommender accurately captures user "vibes" and avoids recommending inappropriate energy levels, though it highlights potential biases toward energy over diversity.

These detailed comparisons demonstrate that the system effectively tests distinct musical preferences: energy drives the biggest shifts, genre creates strong preferences, mood adds nuance, and acoustic liking refines acoustic tracks. The outputs are valid as they align with intuitive musical expectations, though the experiment (doubling energy weight) showed how sensitive the system is to weighting changes, potentially over-emphasizing energy at the expense of other factors.
